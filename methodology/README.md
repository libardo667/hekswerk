# Hekswerk Methodology

A working library of methodology essays from Hekswerk — a relocation systems planning practice based in Portland, Oregon, currently preparing for an international relocation of its own.

## What this is

This repository contains the methodology essays underwriting Hekswerk's advisory practice. Each essay names a discipline — what kind of claim to trust, how to recognize a system, when to draw an arrow, what to do with a circular dependency — and makes the methodology auditable in a way most advisory practices don't bother with.

The essays exist because a methodology that only lives in the practitioner's head doesn't survive contact with real engagements. Writing it down is partly a discipline for the practitioner, partly an institutional memory for the practice, and partly a way for anyone evaluating Hekswerk to verify that the methodology is real rather than rhetorical.

## How to read it

The essays are numbered (M-01 through M-17), but they aren't meant to be read in order. Each essay stands alone and cross-references the others where relevant. The navigation index ([TOC.md](TOC.md)) groups them thematically and shows which are complete and which are still scaffolded.

Every essay carries a status label:

- **v0.1 — Draft (pre-first-engagement)**: completed at v0.1 quality, used in synthetic engagement work, not yet stress-tested by a real third-party client engagement.
- **Scaffold**: titled and outlined; contents in active development.

The library is a living document. After each real engagement, essays will revise. Version-bumps happen quarterly or when a real engagement surfaces enough friction to warrant one.

## Why publish this at all

Three reasons.

**It's how the methodology is verifiable.** Hekswerk charges advisory rates ($350–$8,500 depending on scope) for methodology applied carefully to client situations. A discriminating prospect can read the essays and verify that what they're buying is real thinking, not pseudo-expertise dressed up in deliverable wrappers.

**It serves as institutional memory.** The methodology has been emerging over years; some pieces only exist as habits, not as articulated rules. Writing them down stabilizes them, makes them transferable, and prevents drift. If a second practitioner ever joins, or if the practitioner wants to revisit their own choices a year from now, this is where to come.

**It contributes to a field that lacks coherent methodological tradition.** Relocation advisory exists in a fragmented state — corporate relocation services, immigration attorneys, expat coaches, paperwork-filing services — and there isn't a tradition that takes soft systems (capacity, identity, community, support structure) as seriously as it takes hard ones (legal, tax, logistics). This isn't an attempt to start a school. It's an attempt to make a developed methodology legible enough that someone else doing similar work could borrow from it, argue with it, or build on it.

## Current state (May 2026)

Nine of seventeen planned essays (~53%) are at v0.1 quality. The rest exist as scaffolds. See [TOC.md](TOC.md) for what exists and what's forthcoming.

The methodology is pre-first-engagement: it has been stress-tested through synthetic engagement work and through the practitioner's own ongoing relocation case, but no real third-party client engagement has completed yet. Treat the v0.1 designation accordingly — the methodology is real, but it will revise on contact with real engagement work.

## About Hekswerk

Hekswerk is a relocation systems planning practice serving clients navigating complex international moves — particularly those whose situations involve neurodivergent or chronic-illness capacity considerations, queer/trans identity context, complex partner-coordinated households, or multi-jurisdictional financial complexity.

Hekswerk is operated by Levi Banks (they/them), a self-employed sole proprietor based in Portland, Oregon, currently preparing for a relocation to the Netherlands via the DAFT pathway.
