---

## 1. Why this doc exists

Real relocation plans fail when they assume unlimited energy, executive function, recovery time, attention, and household coordination. D-06's open questions list "client capacity as risk" as an unresolved problem precisely because the obvious version of the language sounds like a diagnosis. "Client may become overwhelmed" reads as a clinical assessment dressed up in spreadsheet vocabulary, and rightly puts clients off. But omitting capacity entirely produces plans that quietly demand the client be someone they are not.

This note gives Hekswerk a respectful language for naming bandwidth as a structural planning constraint. Capacity belongs in the risk register because the plan acts on a human body and household — but the row must locate failure in the plan's design, not in the client's character. Reading time: ~10 minutes. Reference time once internalized: under 60 seconds to audit a draft risk-register row.

---

## 2. Working definition

> *Capacity* is the available practical, cognitive, emotional, and bodily bandwidth a household has to spend on relocation work during a given window. *Capacity risk* is the structural risk that the plan, as currently designed, demands more capacity than the client has available — not a prediction about the client's resilience.

Capacity is not a personal quality; it is a resource the plan consumes. A capacity risk row says, in effect: "the plan is over-budgeting on bandwidth here." The remedy is to redesign the plan, not to expect the client to be more bandwidthful.

This framing matters because it preserves what risk-register thinking is good at — making invisible exposure visible enough to plan around — without doing what risk-register thinking is bad at, which is reducing a human to a list of vulnerabilities.

---

## 3. The non-pathologizing rule

Every capacity row in D-06 must satisfy three constraints. If a row fails any one, rewrite before delivery.

### 1. Failure must be located in the plan, not the client

The row names a property of the plan's design ("September concentrates legal + housing + medical + pet work"), not a property of the client's character ("client may not cope").

*Test*: Reread the row as if the client just received it cold. Does it tell them something about the plan they could change, or something about themselves they cannot?

### 2. No medical or diagnostic inference

Even when the practitioner knows the client lives with AuDHD, chronic illness, anxiety, or other conditions, the client-facing row does not infer or label. It names the structural fact (density peak, simultaneous decision load, sensory exposure) and pairs it with a design response. Diagnostic context belongs in practitioner-only working notes, not in the delivered register.

*Test*: Could this row be lifted out of context and read by a stranger without revealing anything the client did not choose to disclose?

### 3. The row must name a design response, not just an exposure

A capacity row without a mitigation is risk theater. The methodology requires that every capacity row include a concrete plan move: defer, delegate, sequence differently, reduce simultaneity, build a recovery window, externalize to a provider. A row that names load without naming response makes the client feel watched without giving them anything to do.

*Test*: Can the client point to the specific thing in the plan that changes as a result of this row?

## 4. The practitioner-as-mirror frame

The practitioner's job is to reflect the plan back to the client, not to assess the client. The phrasing that follows from this is structural:

- *"The plan currently concentrates X kinds of work in Y window."*
- *"This window asks the household to make Z simultaneous decisions."*
- *"This step assumes the client has W recovery time between major appointments."*

What the phrasing avoids:

- *"The client may not be able to handle…"*
- *"Given the client's history of…"*
- *"There is a risk the client will…"*

The first set names the plan; the second names the client. The same underlying observation can be expressed either way, and the methodology requires the first.

This frame also clarifies what the practitioner *can* see and what the client *must* tell them. The practitioner can see plan structure. The client must surface their actual bandwidth — through intake, through conversation, through the choices they have already made about what to defer, delegate, or simplify. The risk row reflects what both parties already know.

## 5. The decisions the client has already made

A meaningful portion of capacity work is recognizing decisions the client has *already* made to protect their own bandwidth, and naming them as the legitimate planning moves they are. Examples from the L.B. dogfood case (May 2026):

- **Vehicle donation rather than sale.** Reduces the simultaneous load of "managing a transaction during an already-dense departure quarter." The plan reads this as a capacity-respecting simplification, not as money left on the table.
- **Deferring permanent housing search until after arrival.** Recognizes that high-stakes destination housing decisions made from origin are bandwidth-expensive and information-thin. The plan reads this as deliberate sequencing, not as procrastination.
- **Externalizing business address to a domicile service.** Reduces the cognitive load of running personal and business address chains in the same head. See [[m08-reusable-circular-dependencies]] on the dual-address pattern.
- **Pacing decluttering across months rather than weeks.** Recognizes that decluttering is cognitively expensive in a way that scales nonlinearly with compression.
- **Deferring non-acute medical decisions until destination provider relationships exist.** Recognizes that mid-transition medical decision-making operates with less continuity of care than either origin or destination provides.

The risk register should *dignify* these choices by naming them as design moves with reasons, rather than treating them as gaps. A row that reads "client deferred X" sounds like a finding; "the plan defers X to reduce simultaneous load during the density peak" sounds like a strategy.

## 6. Writing a D-06 capacity row

A capacity risk row uses the same template as other D-06 rows — risk, trigger, mitigation, contingency, owner, status — but the wording follows the rules in Section 3.

### Bad row, good row pairs

**Bad**: "Client may become overwhelmed during September."
**Better**: "September–November concentrates legal, housing, medical, pet, and logistics work; plan should pre-decide deferrals and provider delegation before the density peak."

**Bad**: "AuDHD/chronic illness risk."
**Better**: "Capacity-sensitive sequencing required during density peak; protect recovery windows and reduce simultaneous decision load."

**Bad**: "Client has limited bandwidth."
**Better**: "Plan currently assumes 12+ hours/week of client decision time during August–October; if actuals fall below this, defer permanent housing search and consolidate provider conversations."

### Anatomy of a strong capacity row

- **Risk**: structural description of the load the plan places on the client, not a description of the client.
- **Trigger**: a concrete observable that says "we have reached the point where the design assumption is failing." Examples: missed two consecutive provider appointments; decision queue grows beyond five items; sleep or recovery time drops below a stated baseline that the client has named themselves.
- **Mitigation**: the design move that reduces the load — defer, delegate, sequence differently, batch, externalize.
- **Contingency**: what the plan does if the mitigation is insufficient. Often: timeline extension, scope reduction, or provider takeover of a chain.
- **Owner**: usually shared between client and Levi. The client owns the bandwidth signal; the practitioner owns the design response.
- **Status**: standard D-06 vocabulary (active, monitor, mitigated, accepted).

### The "accepted" status, specifically

Some capacity risks are accepted rather than mitigated — the client and practitioner have agreed that the plan asks more than is comfortable but the alternative is worse. The accepted status must be explicit. An unstated accepted risk reads, later, as a missed risk; an explicit one reads as a deliberate choice.

## 7. Density peaks and the timing of capacity load

Capacity risk is not uniform across the move; it concentrates. D-02 already exposes density visually through the timeline. D-06 uses the same density signal to drive capacity rows.

A *density peak* is a window where multiple critical-path systems require active work simultaneously. For a US → NL DAFT move, the typical peak sits in the 8–12 weeks before arrival: legal filings, housing search, document apostille, pet certification, healthcare bridging, and logistics all want attention at once.

The methodology's response to density peaks:

- **Pre-decide deferrals**. Decide before the peak which non-critical work will be paused, rather than triaging under load.
- **Pre-decide delegations**. Identify which provider conversations the client will route through Hekswerk rather than direct, to reduce inbox load during the peak.
- **Protect recovery windows**. Mark specific days or weekends as off-limits to relocation work. These are not luxuries; they are part of the plan's design. A plan without recovery windows is a plan that consumes its own capacity assumptions.
- **Reduce simultaneous decision load**. Where two decisions can be sequenced rather than made in parallel, sequence them — even at modest cost in calendar time. The plan trades calendar for cognitive bandwidth deliberately.

These moves all appear as line items in D-06 and as design constraints in D-02. The methodology's distinguishing claim is that they are *normal planning*, not accommodations.

## 8. Practitioner capacity

Practitioner capacity is real and load-bearing, but it is almost always *internal* — practitioner-only working notes, not client-facing rows. The practitioner-side risk register tracks:

- Engagement concurrency limits (how many active engagements is the practitioner running at this density?).
- Practitioner recovery windows (what happens to engagement quality after consecutive walkthrough days?).
- The practitioner's own capacity-sensitive sequencing (when is deep-work-vs-shallow-work scheduling actively required?).

This material informs pricing, scope, and engagement pacing. It does not go in the client's register. The exception: if a practitioner capacity constraint is going to materially affect the client's plan — a planned absence during a density peak, a known turnaround-time floor — the *fact* (not the underlying reason) is communicated to the client as a planning input.

The methodology takes practitioner capacity seriously because a plan whose author is over capacity produces worse work. Naming it internally is part of doing the work well.

## 9. Adjacent vocabulary

Capacity-related terms used consistently across the methodology:

- **Capacity**: available practical, cognitive, emotional, and bodily bandwidth.
- **Load**: what the plan asks of that capacity in a given window.
- **Density peak**: a window where many systems require simultaneous action.
- **Simultaneous decision load**: the count of unresolved decisions live at once. Distinct from total task count; decisions cost more per unit.
- **Recovery window**: a deliberately reserved span where the plan asks for nothing.
- **Drift**: unmanaged slippage of plan dates because actual capacity fell below modeled capacity.
- **Resequencing**: deliberate, load-aware adjustment of the order of work.
- **Externalization**: shifting a chain to a provider so the chain leaves the client's cognitive surface.

Capacity is not the same as:

- **Resilience**: a character claim. The methodology does not measure or appeal to resilience.
- **Motivation**: also a character claim. The methodology assumes the client is motivated; if they were not, they would not have engaged.
- **Discipline**: ditto.

Plans that lean on resilience, motivation, or discipline as load-bearing assumptions are plans that have hidden their capacity assumptions in the client's character. The methodology surfaces the assumption back into the plan.

## 10. How to use this in practice

**During D-00 intake**: capacity surfaces in two ways — what the client explicitly says about their bandwidth, and what the client has already done to protect it. Both are intake signals. Note them. Do not infer further than what was said.

**During D-02 timeline construction**: read the timeline for density peaks. Every peak should have an explicit capacity strategy attached — pre-decided deferrals, delegations, recovery windows. If a peak has no strategy, the timeline is asking too much of the future client.

**During D-06 register drafting**: run every candidate capacity row through the three rules in Section 3 before drafting the final text. Most first-draft capacity rows fail at least one rule. The "bad row, good row" pairs in Section 6 are the canonical reference.

**During the walkthrough call**: capacity rows are often where clients react most strongly — relief when they feel accurately named, friction when they feel exposed. The non-pathologizing rule predicts which response. If a client flinches at a capacity row, recheck the row against rule 1: is it describing the plan or the client?

**Across engagements**: collect bad-row drafts and what made them bad. The pattern of slippage — where practitioner first-draft language leaks into client-facing text — is the most useful training data this note generates. Studio validation tooling should ideally flag drafted rows that smell pathologizing for review before delivery; see open questions below.

**Pricing implication**: capacity-respecting pacing has a cost — slower calendar, more delegation, more practitioner involvement in chains the client could theoretically run alone. The Roadmap Package pricing should reflect this. A practice that charges as if capacity were unlimited is a practice that quietly punishes clients who use it well.

## 11. Open questions (v0.1)

- **Internal vs. client-facing split.** The cleanest discipline is "practitioner capacity stays internal." But there are cases where the practitioner has read the client more accurately than the client has read themselves; the protocol for surfacing that, if at all, is unsettled. Lean: never in writing; sometimes in conversation, framed as a question.
- **Practitioner capacity as register category.** Should D-06 contain a small internal-only practitioner capacity section, archived but not delivered? Working assumption: yes, but kept in a separate working file rather than in the delivered register to avoid accidental disclosure.
- **Pricing model for capacity-respecting pacing.** Open. Probably a function of total engagement weeks rather than total hours, since capacity-respecting work distributes the same hours across more calendar. Beta clients will pressure-test.
- **Validation tooling.** Can the studio flag drafted register rows that violate the three rules? Likely yes for rule 1 (pathologizing language) using a keyword/pattern check; harder for rules 2 and 3. Worth a first pass during studio validation work; see [[m08-reusable-circular-dependencies]] open questions for the broader pattern-validation conversation.
- **Client-stated bandwidth.** Intake currently asks about bandwidth qualitatively. May need a structured prompt — not as a diagnostic instrument, but as a way to give clients language to describe their own constraints without feeling cornered.
- **Soft systems and capacity overlap.** Capacity sits adjacent to the soft systems named in [[m01-what-counts-as-a-system]] but is not identical to any of them. Worth a future note on how capacity interacts with partner alignment, emotional regulation, and identity work without collapsing them.
- **Acceptance language.** When a capacity risk is "accepted" — the plan asks more than is comfortable, by mutual agreement — the wording must avoid implying that the client volunteered for distress. Current best phrasing under test: "This window is acknowledged as high-load; mitigations in place are X, Y; no further structural reduction available within timeline constraints." Untested with clients.
