---

## 1. Why this doc exists

A provider shortlist is stale the moment it ships. The attorney who had availability in May has filled their calendar by July; the serviced apartment that appeared on three relocation directories disappeared after a single corporate lease; the cross-border tax specialist whose fees were transparent last quarter is now on a waitlist. D-04's value does not come from being an evergreen reference. It comes from narrowing the field, naming evaluation criteria, and setting up the client's next verification step at the moment the engagement needs the information.

The discipline this note specifies is honesty about that. Every D-04 row carries a timestamp and an aging profile, the prose tells the client what aging speed the row sits in, and the practice has a rule for when a shortlist needs refresh. The alternative — pretending the shortlist is a permanent answer — produces clients who circle back four months later angry that "the firm you recommended" no longer takes their case, and produces a practice that quietly accumulates promises it cannot keep.

Reading time: ~10 minutes end-to-end. Reference time once internalized: under 15 seconds per drafted D-04 row.

## 2. Working definition

> *Provider research aging* is the rate at which the specific claims in a D-04 row — availability, pricing, responsiveness, scope, fit — drift from "currently true" toward "no longer true." The aging rate is a property of the category, not of the research effort. *Freshness* is the position of a given row on its aging curve at the moment of delivery, expressed as a "last checked" date and an aging-speed label.

The discipline composes with confidence labels (see [[m06-confidence-labels]]) and with authority verification (see [[m13-verify-with-authority]]). A confirmed provider availability from last week is high-confidence today, medium-confidence next month, and a candidate for refresh by the following quarter — even though nothing in the row's content changed. Aging is not an error mode; it is a feature of the information itself.

The practice's job is to make the aging visible and to design product around it. The client's job is to act on the research within the freshness window, or to commission a refresh if they delay.

## 3. Aging-speed categories

Provider research falls into three broad aging-speed bands. Every D-04 row should be classified into one and tagged accordingly.

### Fast (days to weeks)

Claims about *individual capacity, current inventory, and immediate availability*. These change faster than any reasonable refresh cadence the practice can include in a Roadmap Package.

*Examples*:

- Short-term and serviced housing inventory at a specific destination, especially in tight markets and high seasons.
- An individual attorney's, accountant's, or specialist's availability for new clients.
- Provider quotes for time-sensitive services (mover slots, vet appointments tied to import windows).
- Waitlist length for a specific GP, therapist, or specialist.

*Rule*: fast-aging rows must be delivered with an explicit "verify availability before relying on this" instruction, and the client should be advised to act within days, not weeks, of delivery. The practice does not guarantee these claims past the delivery week.

### Medium (weeks to months)

Claims about *named-provider competence, scope, pricing structure, and language of operation*. These can change but typically do not within a single Roadmap Package engagement.

*Examples*:

- A specific immigration counsel's stated practice areas and pathway experience.
- An accountant's fee structure and engagement model.
- An international mover's stated service scope and customs handling.
- A pet relocation provider's stated routes and partner network.
- A bank's documented onboarding requirements for non-resident accounts.

*Rule*: medium-aging rows carry a "last checked" date and should be considered current for roughly one engagement cycle (i.e., the active Roadmap Package window). If the client returns six months later for Implementation Support, the row needs a refresh check before being acted on.

### Slow (months to years)

Claims about *established provider categories, official body identities, regulatory frameworks, and category-level fit criteria*. These shift on a timescale longer than typical engagements.

*Examples*:

- The existence and remit of a specific government office, regulator, or registry.
- The general category of "you need cross-border tax advice if X, Y, Z" for a given pathway.
- The presence of a credential-evaluation industry for regulated professions.
- The general structure of the destination's healthcare insurance market.
- Identity-specific community resource categories (the resources change; the category persists).

*Rule*: slow-aging claims can be reused across engagements with light refresh. They are also the claims that benefit most from the practice's compounding archive — verified once with named authority, they remain useful background for many cases.

## 4. The freshness expression

Every D-04 row, in both the matrix and the category notes, expresses freshness explicitly. Three pieces of information together:

1. **Last checked date** — the date on which the practitioner most recently verified the specific claims in the row.
2. **Aging speed label** — fast, medium, or slow.
3. **Verification path** — what the client should do to confirm currency before acting (a one-line instruction, e.g., *"call to confirm availability and current intake list"*).

In prose, the freshness expression is small and direct:

> *"Researched May 14, 2026 (medium-aging). Verify intake availability by phone before scheduling a consult — counsel availability for new Blue Card cases can shift within a quarter."*

The client does not have to learn a vocabulary to use this. The labels are common-sense; the verification path is action-shaped. The discipline is to *include* them — not to invent jargon around them.

## 5. Worked examples

From the Mason Kirsch dogfood test case (single adult, Atlanta → Berlin, Blue Card pathway, target August 2026 arrival, lease ends June 30):

### Fast-aging row: Berlin short-term housing

> *Provider: [serviced apartment operator name]. Category: short-term destination housing. Researched May 14, 2026 (fast-aging — Berlin summer inventory turns within days). Apparently relevant because: registration-eligible address, 1–3 month leases, central districts. Verification path: check live availability on operator site for arrival week before relying on this row; if no availability, route to backup providers in same matrix section.*

Aging speed warning is load-bearing. Berlin summer (July–September) housing market is volatile; a row researched in May cannot promise anything about August inventory. The client should know this before they relax.

### Medium-aging row: immigration counsel

> *Provider: [German immigration counsel firm name]. Category: Blue Card legal support. Researched May 14, 2026 (medium-aging). Stated practice areas include EU Blue Card; firm appears to handle employer-sponsored cases with a regular cadence. Fee structure on website. Verification path: confirm current intake availability and fee schedule directly; ask whether they handle the specific consulate handling Mason's case.*

This row is useful for several months. The firm's competence and scope are unlikely to evaporate; their availability for a new client might. The verification path tells the client what to confirm and what to ignore.

### Medium-aging row: cross-border tax (RSU question)

> *Provider: [US/Germany cross-border tax specialist or firm]. Category: cross-border tax — specifically, equity compensation across a US-to-Germany move. Researched May 14, 2026 (medium-aging). Fit notes: Mason's RSU vest on July 15, 2026 sits in the move window and has tax implications under both jurisdictions; this category is needed and time-sensitive. Verification path: confirm specialist actually handles RSU/equity treatment, not just expat earned-income returns; confirm engagement timeline can produce guidance before July 15.*

Specialist competence is medium-aging; the *timing* of engagement is fast-aging (Mason needs guidance before July 15). The row expresses both clearly.

### Slow-aging claim: category framing

> *Category: cross-border tax. Why this category matters: any US person becoming tax-resident in Germany faces dual filing obligations and treaty interaction. Cross-border tax advice is a standard category for US→Germany moves regardless of income complexity.*

This claim does not need to be re-verified per engagement. It is part of the practice's accumulating category knowledge and is safely reused. The specific provider rows beneath it carry their own freshness expressions.

### Counter-example: a row that should not have been delivered

> *Provider: [housing operator]. Researched March 2026. Two months old at delivery, fast-aging category, no verification path included.*

This row is aged past usefulness. The practitioner should have refreshed it before D-04 shipped, or marked it as a stale lead worth checking before relying on. Shipping it without acknowledgment is the failure mode this note exists to prevent.

## 6. When a shortlist needs refresh

Refresh triggers, in order of how often they fire in practice:

1. **Time-based.** A shortlist older than its fastest-aging row's freshness window should not be acted on without a refresh check. For a shortlist whose fastest row is a Berlin summer housing operator, the freshness window is on the order of days; for an immigration counsel shortlist in a stable market, weeks to a couple of months.
2. **Engagement-state change.** Pathway change, destination change, household composition change, or material timeline shift all invalidate categories of the shortlist. The matrix may not all need rebuilding — slow-aging claims survive — but every row touching the changed dimension needs re-verification.
3. **Client-reported friction.** The client contacts a shortlisted provider and finds them unresponsive, unavailable, out of scope, or no longer matching the row's claims. This is the most valuable refresh signal because it is real-world feedback; it should be logged and the row updated or removed.
4. **Practice-side signal.** The practitioner learns through another engagement, a provider's own announcement, or a regulatory change that a specific row's claims have shifted. The row gets updated even if no current client has flagged it; the next client benefits from the correction.

Refresh discipline matters specifically for clients (like Mason) whose timeline puts D-04 use weeks or months downstream of D-04 delivery. The Roadmap Package's pricing should reflect that the shortlist is research at *this* point in time, not a standing service.

## 7. The boundary between D-04 and Implementation Support

D-04 produces a fresh shortlist at one point in time. *Maintaining* that shortlist past the Roadmap Package window — verifying continued availability, swapping in replacements when providers drop off, updating fee structures, monitoring for category-level changes — is a different service.

The methodology's working boundary:

- **In Roadmap Package scope**: a single D-04 with freshness labels; one revision pass during the engagement; the shortlist is current at delivery and within a defined window (working default: 30 days from D-04 delivery for medium-aging rows; act-immediately for fast-aging rows).
- **In Implementation Support scope**: refresh of the shortlist on a defined cadence (working default: monthly for active categories, quarterly for monitor categories); response to client-reported friction; addition of new candidates as needs surface.
- **Out of scope entirely**: provider management. The client always hires, supervises, pays, and fires their own providers. Hekswerk does not stand between client and provider.

The line between "shipped fresh and aged out gracefully" and "actively maintained" is what justifies separating the two product lines. A Roadmap Package that quietly assumes the shortlist will stay fresh is mispriced and overpromises.

## 8. Adjacent vocabulary

- **Aging speed**: the rate at which a row's claims drift from true to stale. Property of the category, not the research effort.
- **Freshness**: position on the aging curve at delivery; expressed as last-checked date + aging label.
- **Refresh**: re-verification of an aged row, partial or full.
- **Verification path**: the one-line action the client should take to confirm currency before relying on the row. Pairs with [[m13-verify-with-authority]].
- **Compounding archive**: slow-aging category knowledge accumulated across engagements; reusable across clients with light refresh.
- **Endorsement creep**: presenting a researched shortlist as a guaranteed recommendation. A separate discipline failure, not aging; see [[m10-endorsement-creep]] when that note exists.

Provider research is not the same as provider endorsement. Aging discipline tells the client *when* a researched candidate was true; endorsement discipline tells them that "researched" is not "vouched for." The two compose: a fresh shortlist with weak endorsement language is honest research; a stale shortlist with strong endorsement language is the worst combination.

## 9. How to use this in practice

**During D-04 research**: log the date of each verification action as you go. A row whose research dates span a six-week window is not "researched on the delivery date" — it is a composite of older and newer claims, and the row's last-checked date is the oldest verification it contains.

**During D-04 drafting**: classify every row into fast / medium / slow aging. If you cannot classify, the row is probably underspecified — what exactly are you claiming, and how would you know if it stopped being true? Re-research before shipping.

**In the matrix itself**: include columns for "last checked" and "aging speed." In the prose category notes, restate the aging implications in plain language. The matrix is for reference; the prose is for orientation.

**In the cover prose**: name the freshness window in the D-04 introduction. *"This shortlist is current as of [date]. Fast-aging rows (marked) should be acted on within days; medium-aging rows within roughly a month. After that, individual rows may need to be reverified before contacting providers."* The client should leave the document with calibrated expectations, not a vague sense that "Levi looked these up."

**During the walkthrough**: explicitly point at fast-aging rows. Mention which categories are time-pressured and what verification steps the client should take this week vs. next month. Clients who internalize the aging will not feel betrayed when a row goes stale; clients who do not will.

**After delivery**: log client-reported friction. A row that produces "they were unresponsive" or "they don't take this pathway" feedback gets updated in the practice's working files. Over time, the freshness of repeated rows is calibrated by lived signal, not just by recency.

**Across engagements**: the compounding archive (slow-aging category knowledge, verified category structures, known-good intake question banks for providers) is the practice's asset. Build it deliberately. Fast-aging row content does not compound; aging discipline itself does.

## 10. Open questions (v0.1)

- **"Last checked" field**: whether every D-04 row needs a per-row last-checked date or whether a per-section date suffices. Lean: per-row, because aging speed varies within a section.
- **Default freshness window**: the working "act within 30 days for medium-aging rows" rule is unvalidated. Worth tracking actuals for the first three to five engagements and revising.
- **Refresh service**: whether Implementation Support should include a standing monthly refresh of the shortlist, an on-demand refresh on client trigger, or both. Probably both, priced separately.
- **Affiliate / commission policy interaction**: aging discipline is honest about *when* a row was true; endorsement discipline is honest about *how* the practitioner relates to the provider. Both need a written policy that survives across engagements. See [[m10-endorsement-creep]] when that note exists.
- **Mason-shaped clients**: thorough, regulatory-text-comfortable clients may want to verify everything themselves. The aging expression should welcome this — the verification path is a starting point for the client's own due diligence, not a substitute for it. Test whether such clients find the freshness labels useful scaffolding or condescending overlay.
- **Aging archive vs. case archive**: the practice's compounding archive (slow-aging category knowledge) lives separately from per-engagement case files. The data structure for promoting verified slow-aging claims from a case archive into the shared archive is unsettled. Worth pinning down before the second engagement adds the same category knowledge a second time without reuse.
