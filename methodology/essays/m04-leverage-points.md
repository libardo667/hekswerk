---

## 1. Why this doc exists

D-01 asks the practitioner to name three to five *leverage points*, and D-02 then uses those leverage points as the spine of the timeline. The spec for D-01 does not yet discipline the judgment call. Without criteria, the category drifts: into "the decisions the client is most anxious about," into "the items with the largest line in the budget," into "whatever I happened to write down first." This note makes leverage points operational enough to use during D-01 drafting, defensible enough to explain during a walkthrough, and stable enough that two different drafts of the same engagement would name roughly the same handful.

Reading time: ~10 minutes end-to-end. Reference time once internalized: under 30 seconds for a single judgment call.

---

## 2. Working definition

> A *leverage point* is a decision or resolution point whose outcome changes the shape of multiple downstream systems. It is not just emotionally salient, expensive, or urgent. It earns the label because movement there collapses branches, reduces uncertainty, or changes the operating logic of the rest of the engagement.

The test is structural, not psychological. A decision that consumes a great deal of the client's attention but only resolves into one downstream path is not a leverage point — it is an important decision. A decision that quietly reorganizes three other systems once it lands is a leverage point even if nobody is losing sleep over it.

Leverage points are how the methodology tells the client what to focus on, in what order, with the smallest plausible cognitive load. They are also how Hekswerk earns its fee: identifying the right three or four points and unsticking them is much of the value of the Map.

## 3. The six-criterion checklist

Apply in the moment, mentally or on paper. If a candidate passes 3+, it's probably a leverage point. 5+ and it definitely belongs in D-01 Section 4.

### 1. Downstream connectedness

The decision touches at least three other systems in a load-bearing way. Not "everything is connected" — specifically: three or more other systems would have to react to a change here.

*Test*: Name the systems that have to revise their plans once this lands. If you can name fewer than three, it is probably a within-system decision.

### 2. Branch reduction

Resolution removes live options from the planning space. Before the decision there were several viable futures; after, there are fewer. The map becomes simpler.

*Test*: Can you name at least two branches that disappear once this is resolved? If the answer is "the same branches remain, we just know more about them," it is a research question, not a leverage point.

### 3. Decision window

There is a meaningful time band during which the decision can be made well, and that band is narrower than the engagement. Decisions with infinite windows accumulate but rarely drive sequencing; decisions with three-day windows are emergencies, not leverage points. Leverage points sit in the middle.

*Test*: When does the decision get expensive to defer? If "never" or "tomorrow," it does not belong.

### 4. External actor or constraint involvement

Resolving the decision requires at least one actor or constraint outside the client's direct control: an agency, a market, a regulator, a biological clock, a counterparty. Pure preference decisions ("which color suitcase?") rarely have leverage; the world has to weigh in.

*Test*: Who or what besides the client has to say yes — or what has to be true in the world — for this to resolve?

### 5. Sequencing power

The decision controls when other work can begin. Until it lands, downstream systems are either blocked or operating on speculation. Once it lands, work that was waiting can move.

*Test*: Which other systems are currently in a holding pattern because of this? If none, it has no sequencing power, regardless of how important it feels.

### 6. Strategy-shaping (not just execution)

The decision changes *what kind of plan the client is on*, not just *how the plan is executed*. Pathway choice, housing strategy, and capital structure shape strategy. Booking a specific appointment within an already-chosen pathway is execution.

*Test*: If this resolves one way versus another, do other systems have meaningfully different shapes — or do they have the same shape with different details?

## 4. Worked examples

Examples drawn from the L.B. dogfood case (US → NL, DAFT pathway, May 2026 cohort).

### Clear leverage points (pass 5–6 criteria)

**DAFT filing sequence.**

- Connectedness: gates KVK, business banking, capital deposit, BSN, residence card, housing options. ✓✓
- Branch reduction: until the filing strategy is set, the engagement is running two parallel plans (DAFT-first vs. address-first). ✓
- Decision window: weeks, not months, once the attorney is engaged. ✓
- External actors: IND, KVK, business bank, attorney. ✓
- Sequencing power: most other systems wait or hedge until this lands. ✓
- Strategy: yes — alters the entire architecture of the move. ✓
- **Verdict**: Leverage point. The spine of the timeline.

**Origin home disposition (sell vs. rent vs. hold).**

- Connectedness: capital available for DAFT deposit, tax position, departure date flexibility, emotional pacing, US-side property as ongoing system or not. ✓
- Branch reduction: collapses three live futures into one. ✓
- Decision window: tied to the housing market and to lease/sale cycles — months, not weeks. ✓
- External actors: realtor, market, possible tenants, tax advisor. ✓
- Sequencing power: gates capital deployment, gates emotional bandwidth for packing, gates whether US property persists as a system post-move. ✓
- Strategy: yes — different downstream architectures for each branch. ✓
- **Verdict**: Leverage point.

### Resolved leverage points

**Pet transport routing (surface transit decision, resolved March 2026).**

- Before resolution, the case had three live travel-mode branches (cargo air, cabin, surface). Each implied different timing, different vet certification chains, different cost ranges, and different stress profiles. Once surface transit was chosen, those branches collapsed and several downstream systems (travel-day plan, vet schedule, housing arrival window) could resolve to specifics.
- **Verdict**: Leverage point at the time of decision; now resolved. Worth keeping visible on the Map as a teaching artifact during the walkthrough, but no longer active in D-02 sequencing.

### Edge cases

**Destination neighborhood selection.**

For a single adult on DAFT with no children, this is usually a within-system decision inside *Housing — destination acquisition* — it shapes execution but does not reorganize other systems. For a family with school-age children, the same decision drives school enrollment, transit, language environment, and community fit, and easily passes 5/6 criteria. Same decision, different leverage profile depending on household composition. The criteria flex; the rule does not.

**Visa pathway choice (when client is ranging).**

When a client is still choosing between DAFT, Highly Skilled Migrant, and family reunification, pathway choice is the highest-leverage decision in the engagement — it determines which systems exist at all. Once the pathway is chosen, it stops being a leverage point and becomes the *premise* of the engagement, even though it remains highly important. Leverage points are decision points; premises are the ground the decisions stand on.

### Things that look like leverage points but aren't

**Suitcase purchase.**

- Connectedness: weak. Affects packing and that's about it.
- Branch reduction: none meaningful.
- Decision window: wide; can be made the week before departure.
- **Verdict**: Important task. Lives inside *Physical Logistics*. Not on the Map.

**Choosing a specific attorney from the shortlist.**

Often feels weighty because money and trust are involved, but the choice typically does not change the shape of downstream systems — it changes who executes within the same architecture. Strong execution decision; not a leverage point.

*Exception*: if the attorney choice is between specialists who would actually pursue different pathways or strategies, then it inherits the leverage of the pathway question. This is rare but worth checking.

**"What if we delay arrival by a quarter?"**

This feels like a leverage decision because the timeline is the most visible artifact in the engagement. But a uniform shift of the whole plan rarely passes criterion 2 (branch reduction) or criterion 6 (strategy): same architecture, slid right on the calendar. It is a parameter change, not a leverage point — unless the shift crosses a hard external boundary (visa expiry, school year, lease cliff), in which case it triggers branch reduction and earns the label.

## 5. The lifecycle of a leverage point

Leverage points are not static. A drafted Map should distinguish between:

- **Active** — the decision is live and unresolved. Belongs in D-01 Section 4 prominently, and drives D-02 sequencing.
- **Resolved** — the decision has been made well enough to plan around. Keep it visible during walkthroughs as a teaching artifact, but it no longer drives sequencing. In the studio data model, this is the place where case data and view data diverge — the case still remembers, the view can quietly demote.
- **Demoted** — on closer analysis, the candidate failed the criteria and was downgraded to important decision, task, or research question. Worth keeping a brief note in working files about why, in case the question re-emerges later.
- **Replaced** — a new piece of information made an old leverage point obsolete and surfaced a different one. Common when a provider conversation reveals a constraint the practitioner did not know about.

A useful discipline: when a leverage point resolves, name the next one explicitly rather than letting the Map quietly shrink. Engagements lose their spine when resolved leverage points are not replaced and the practitioner stops navigating from a small, named set.

## 6. The gut heuristic and its complement

### The gut heuristic

*"If this slips, what else moves?"*

Sit with the candidate decision and mentally delete it from the calendar by a month. Which other systems flinch? If three or more systems have to revise their plans, the decision has leverage. If nothing flinches, it does not — no matter how stressful the decision feels.

### Its blind spot

Decisions that are heavy but self-contained. Selling the family piano, for example, can be enormously emotionally costly and require real planning attention without affecting any other system. The gut may want to label it a leverage point because it *feels* heavy. The criteria correctly do not.

### The complement test

*"What does the rest of the plan currently assume about this?"*

If half the plan is operating on a hidden assumption about how this decision will land, the decision has leverage — even if nobody has said so out loud. Hidden assumptions are how leverage gets missed: the practitioner has quietly committed to a branch without noticing, and the work is being done in its shadow. Surfacing the assumption is part of the value.

## 7. Adjacent vocabulary

A leverage point is not the same as:

- **Anchor**: a fixed date or condition that other planning orbits. Anchors do not require a decision; they require navigation.
- **Gate**: a required pass/fail step. Gates are binary and externally controlled; leverage points are choices that reshape the plan.
- **Risk**: an exposure to a bad outcome. Risks are managed; leverage points are decided.
- **Critical-path item**: a step whose timing determines overall schedule. Some leverage points sit on the critical path; some critical-path items are pure execution and have no leverage.
- **Important decision**: a decision that matters but does not reorganize the plan. Leverage points are a strict subset.

Tasks live inside systems. Decisions live inside systems and sometimes between them. Leverage points are decisions that operate *across* systems — that is what distinguishes them.

## 8. Granularity rules

How many leverage points should D-01 name? The spec says three to five. In practice:

- **Three** is the comfortable floor. Below three, the Map is either trivially simple (in which case the client probably does not need a full Roadmap) or the practitioner has not looked hard enough.
- **Five** is the ceiling for a single delivered Map. Beyond five, the client cannot hold them, and the leverage concept loses its discriminating power — it starts to mean "things I should pay attention to," which is what the rest of the Map already says.
- **Above five**: stack-rank and present the top five. Keep the others in working notes as candidates in case a top-five resolves and a slot opens.

The count flexes with engagement complexity but should not exceed five at delivery. If the practitioner cannot pick five from a long candidate list, that is a signal the criteria are not being applied — not a signal to show all of them.

For ranging clients (multiple destinations under consideration), pathway/destination selection is almost always leverage point #1, and the rest are conditional. Note them as such in Section 4.

## 9. How to use this in practice

**During Phase 1 intake**: Listen for the decisions the client is hedging — the ones where they keep saying "well, it depends on…". Hedging is often the gut signaling that downstream work is being held hostage to an unresolved decision. Note candidates; do not name them as leverage points to the client yet.

**During D-01 Section 4 drafting**: Run the six-criterion checklist explicitly for each candidate. For each named leverage point, write one sentence per criterion in working notes. The exercise will demote some candidates and elevate others. Do this before drafting the public-facing paragraph.

**During D-02 timeline construction**: Each named leverage point should appear as a critical-path item with a "latest safe decision" window. If a leverage point cannot be placed on the timeline, either it is not really a leverage point (no decision window — see criterion 3) or the timeline is missing a system.

**During D-03 dependency mapping**: Leverage points correlate with high-outgoing-arrow nodes in the dependency graph. If a named leverage point has fewer than three outgoing dependencies, recheck criterion 1 — connectedness may have been overstated. If a node has many outgoing dependencies but was not named a leverage point, ask why.

**During the walkthrough call**: Name the leverage points plainly. "These three decisions, once made, simplify almost everything else. Most of the rest of the plan is downstream of these." Do not show the client the checklist; trust the work. If they push back on a named leverage point, the criteria give you a defensible explanation in plain language.

**Across engagements**: Track which named leverage points resolved cleanly, which resolved into surprise, and which were quietly demoted mid-engagement. The pattern of misses is the most valuable training data the practice generates. See also [[m08-reusable-circular-dependencies]] — circular dependencies often hide leverage points by making them look unresolvable.

## 10. Open questions (v0.1)

- **Count discipline.** The "three to five" rule is intuitive but untested. Track actuals across the first three to five engagements. If real Maps consistently name two or six, revise.
- **Soft-system leverage.** Can a soft system contain a leverage point? Working assumption: yes — partner alignment, in particular, can be a leverage point in households where the partner is hedging — but soft-system leverage points may need a distinct visual treatment so the Map does not appear to weigh emotional decisions equally with bureaucratic ones, even though the methodology says they belong on the same plane. Test in practice.
- **Visual treatment of resolved leverage points.** Currently planning a "dimmed but present" treatment in the visual map, with the case data retaining full history. May need to test whether dimming reads as "demoted" or as "achieved." See [[m01-what-counts-as-a-system]] on the case-vs-view distinction.
- **Criterion weighting.** All six criteria currently count equally. Suspicion: sequencing power (criterion 5) is the strongest single predictor of leverage; decision window (criterion 3) is the weakest, because most decisions have windows of some kind.
- **Replacement discipline.** When a leverage point resolves, the practitioner should name the next one. Whether that should be a formal D-01 revision trigger or just a working-note discipline is unsettled. Lean: working-note for now, formal revision only if three or more resolve.
